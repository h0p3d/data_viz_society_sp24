Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Starting Question

"How have population numbers and age demographics in the US changed between 1900 and 2000?"

From looking at the numbers and knowing a bit about US history, I anticipated that the data would show both that the US population grew significantly between 1900 and 2000 and that the median life expectancy increased significantly due to lowering infant mortality rates and longer-average life expectancies due to advancements in medical technology among other things. I wanted this visualization to tell that story in a way that humanized or emphasized this dramatic change, but I was torn between whether to focus on one or both narratives.

I chose to ignore the gendered aspect of the data because I do not think that gender is a relevant part of this narrative. Also, there are problems inherent to treating gender like a binary classification instead of as a complex biological, social, and psychological multi-dimensional spectrum. However, age also has a lot of complex biological, social, and ethical meanings (ie., how people physically mature at different rates and experience different biological stages of life at different ages, when is a person considered a child vs. an adult, does life begin at birth, etc.)

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • The idea for this visualization came from the similar graphic we saw about income and college attendance rates in lecture on Monday. I liked the idea of making the data more memorable by having people draw out their guess before getting the answer. The thick black line was my actual guess, I was surprised that the actual numbers for the 2000 census dipped and then increased, but this makes sense due to the baby boomer generation. I think the surprise factor is nice, but I think I also agree that because people don't expect visualizations to be interactive / may not want to put in the effort to explore the visualization that presenting the information up front or in a more "scrolly-telling" style might be more effective.
  • Showing the y-axis in terms of millions instead of thousands of thousands as shown in the raw data made the scale more easily interpretable in my opinion.
  • Note I made this sketch before I saw the phase 2 restriction that the visualiation had to be static.
  • Another limitation is that this is showing the data as if it were continuous when it is really discrete. A bar chart might show this more transparently.
  • The colors for this chart don't really come through (I only had a four color pen), but I wanted the other guesses to be grey, your guess to be black, and the actual numbers to be in a thick blue line. I think using different neutral colors to distinguish the different data points will help highlight the different sources to the user.
  • After showing this prototype to someone with no context for the assignment, the person suggested moving the instructions from a tooltip to above the graphic, moving the legend to the left hand side or the bottom, and commented that they mainly got how close the guess was to the actual from the visualization, and didn't understand why the 1900 data was present. So intent was not achieved.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • I wanted to create a more memorable visualization without requiring as much effort from the user. The conversations about chart junk in lecture made me wonder if adding embellishment like having babies represent the height of the bar chart for age 0, children for age 5, 10, 15, adults for 20-50, and an elderly person for 50+ might emphasize the human effect that decreasing infant mortality and increasing life expectancy had. I even thought it might be cool to embellish the figures to have period dress or be more diverse in terms of hairstyle, gender, and race, or to dress like the decade that group of people was born in but I think that might be more distracting and complicated.
  • The title "A Snapshot of US Population by age 1900 vs 2000" was supposed to evoke emotion of looking at two pictures in the same place side by side a hundred years apart.
  • Adding the decade each cohort was born in was also meant as a neat reverse timeline. I think it both acts as an explainer (the bump in the middle due to the baby boomer generation becomes instantly recognizable by the year) and as a humanizing effect (you start to wonder wow someone who was 40 in 1900 was born during the US Civil War, I wonder what that was like) and it makes the history element more tangible (like wow many people in 1900 still remembered what it was like to live in a US where it was legal to own people.)
  • I think the limitation here is that showing the raw numbers draws your attention to the numbers and the population growth and the history while perhaps de-emphasizing the other narrative of decreasing infant mortality and increased longevity. Another change I might make in hindsight is flipping the x and y axis so that the people are side-by-side, not standing on top of each other.
  • After showing this prototype to someone with no context for the assignment: the person was confused by the description on the left (I accidentally flipped the colors on this chart) and didn't really get that the data was coming from different censuses based on the phrasing. The presence of both decades and age was similarly confusing, and they recommended I go with one or the other (I'm thinking maybe some annotations might make this more clear because including decade of people born in x decade alive in y census year is a bit confusing.)

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • Sorry for the bad bar chart drawing! With this prototype I wanted to explore what would happen if I tried to take the change in population numbers out of the equation so the viewer could compare the age distributions more easily. I think this primarily has the effect of de-emphasizing the population numbers (less transparency?) while also making the difference in age distribution clearer.
  • After showing this prototype to someone with no context for the assignment, the person initially thought it was showing numbers not percentages (but they had also seen the previous two prototypes first so may have been primed to look for numbers), and didn't get the main observation I was trying to convey of changing infant mortality / life expectancy.
  • I think this chart would be better with 10 bins instead of 20, with the bar charts overlapping to allow for better direct comparison, and with annotations at the key points indicating change in infant mortality, median life expectancy, and percent of population above 60.

Reflection

I know this wasn't part of the assignment per se, but I think my main reflection is on the stark contrast between my intent for each prototype visualization and the actual confusion / other feedback that a non-technical user had. I think partly this is due to the rough nature of prototype sketches that the eye immediately jumps to what is unclear or what is not designed well instead of the data itself. But also, I realized that in my first couple of prototypes I was trying to communicate a lot of information and that without the additional context of annotations or explanatory text that the message I was trying to convey was lost. While the first couple of prototypes may have been more "creative", I think I will ultimately make something closer to the third prototype even though it is a bit "boring" and narrower in scope because I think that with simplicity there comes a clarity of information that. Before this, I did not fully appreciate that there is something worse than a visualization that fails to communicate in a memorable or impactful way- it's a visualization that fails to communicate at all.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

When I initially looked at the data on Monday, I had no idea how I was going to approach this assignment. After all, US Census data seems so boring. However, I think the lectures the past couple of weeks on chart annotations, storytelling, affect, and creative interactions helped me frame my design choices around a story or message I wanted to convey. In the previous data exploration assignment, I tried to discover a story in the data and explore how the readings did or did not apply to Boston specific data. This assignment, because I drew the prototypes before deeply analyzing the data, I had to start with a story in mind about population growth and life expectancy improvements, which led me to think and experiment with various techniques that I had seen in lecture or articles that could convey this story and make it memorable.

Ultimately, I decided to answer a question about the difference in age demographics in the US in 1900 and 2000. I chose to use a bar chart because the data had discrete values. I originally had 10 bars instead of 20, but I discovered that the child mortality and aging population statistics I wanted to use fell at 5 years and 65 years so I split the data back into 20 bins, even though this made the x-axis labels a bit tight and cluttered. The 5 year bins also provide a more detailed view of the varying curve of the 2000 data and the normal distribution shape of the 1900 data. In terms of color, I used orange for 1900 as a sort of "warning color" because of the high infant mortality and lower life expectancy, and blue for 2000 provided a clear contrast. In terms of axes, I wanted to provide clear and easy to read interpretations of the data, hence the color legend at the top, the brief y-axis description, and age bins clearly defined so their values are clear. Because the x axis was so cluttered, I added the icons to make it easy to see at a glance what it was trying to convey. Finally, in order to make sure my message / story was clearly conveyed, I flagged points of interest I wanted the viewer to notice with annotations that looked like flags so that the viewer could interpret the chart within the relevant context I was aiming for.

Reflection

Phase one definitely narrowed my scope for this assignment and allowed me to focus my designs towards conveying a specific message. I felt like being primed with examples of engaging visualizations in class and thinking of what my prototypes could look like in the back of my mind for a few days before I even drew them really helped my creative process, and I definitely agree that drawing it on paper helped me avoid being confined in terms of technological limitations / default design choices. Doing the informal user feedback session on my sketches allowed me to iterate quickly and focus on prototype 3, as I realized that trying to convey both growth and demographics might overcomplicate the narrative. The feedback session also confirmed the need for annotations to clearly convey my narrative, as it became clear that raw numbers have many interpretations and not everyone immediately jumps to the historical context of the data. I also added the pictoral elements of prototype 2 to humanize the data a bit. While I was interested in the Baby Boomer bump, I ultimately left out the population growth narrative I explored in prototype 1 and 2 to avoid cluttering the visualization with too many annotations.

Meta-Reflection

I think the past two assignments have been valuable practical learning opportunities, but they feel like two incomplete halves of a larger data visualization design process that I'm starting to get a feel for. I want to start with data exploration, and once I understand the data create a story around it, create visualizations that convey that story and then iterate on those designs based on user feedback. However, implementing the exploration and story building (and seeking my own user feedback) separately helped me understand why it's not as satisfying to have one without the other. I'm afraid that in this assignment, because I was so pre-occupied with my pre-conceived narrative, that I lost out on other insights or other stories the data could have told. It would be interesting to see how my peers interpreted the same data.
