Proposition: Incidents of police misconduct in NYC have been increasing over time and disproportionately affect people of color.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I chose to display the raw number of reports per year. The numbers are increasing likely because not as many 2020 active police officers were working in the early 2000s, which is highly deceptive (-2). Even if the caption mentions active police officers, and cites the source of the data, displaying the data by year is still highly deceptive because most readers won't investigate further.
  • I chose to display the reports by race in decreasing order and included many categories (while not including null, other, refused, and Native American), included different types of allegations, attempted to use a neutral color scheme and stretched the x-axis to make it seem like my intent was informative rather than persuasive. (-1)
  • I obscured the outcome of the complaints and used terms like reports and misconduct in the titles to imply that allegations had been substantiated. While I think filing a complaint is somewhat synonymous with filing a report in some contexts, it is still misleading (-1).

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • In this case, focusing on the number of unvalidated reports per year is highly deceptive, as it makes it seem like the number of false reprts has dramatically increased since the early 2000s. Separating the three possible outcomes (exonerated, unsubstantiated, and substantiated) into two binary categories and omitting how many unsubstantiated complaints are caused by lack of police cooperation is highly deceptive (-2)
  • I left out annotations because I wanted to persuade the reader by letting them make the observations on their own that while white officers had far more allegations, they were more likely to be found innocent compared to black officers (while ommitting mentions of racism at play in both the allegations and their outcomes) is deceptive. (-1)
  • Again, I used a neutral color palette and a variety of chart types to attempt to obscure the persuasive intent of the graphics (-1).

Final Reflection

Creating deceptive visualizations was far harder than I thought it would be. I went through several iterations of graphics, starting with very clearly one-sided visuals that I attempted to tone down by playing with the colors, axes and removing the annotations (see early prototypes below).

I was surprised that both including and ommitting complaint outcome data felt deceptive because of the bias within the data itself. According to the ProPublica description, reports were often unsubstantiated because of police departments were unwilling to provide evidence such as body cam footage. Additionally, current laws give police broad discretion, especially as it applies to use of force which contributes towards why so few incidents are substantiated. Finally, I knew the data was biased towards increasing numbers of reports over time because it only included active police officers in 2020. Because data was not available for all reports over the time period, such as reports that led to police resigning, quitting, or being tried for crimes which would make them not active, the entire framing of the proposition itself was biased. Without the full context for the data it is hard to imagine readers being able to detect all forms of deception. It is also quite easy to imagine visual designers unintentionally creating deceptive visualizations by failing to account for all possible forms of bias within the data.

Given that I cannot erase the bias from incomplete data that is collected within the historically (and current) racist context of policing, I don't think it's possible to create a purely neutral visualization. Certainly more ethical choices could be made that convey the limitations of the data more transparently. Perhaps clearly communicating the intent of the visualization as shown below could in some ways make the bias more transparent, although this likely makes the visualizations far less persuasive.
